Background and Aim

In the third phase of this project, we deployed the ML-informed Logistic Regression (LR) — a hybrid development methodology that uses Machine Learning (ML) techniques during the feature engineering phase to discover which non-linear effects and feature interactions carry genuine predictive signal. Those discovered patterns were then encoded as explicit, fixed terms inside a production logistic regression, preserving full coefficient transparency and regulatory auditability.

In principle, this approach gives us the best of both worlds: The feature engineering intelligence of ML combined with the regulatory defensibility of a traditional scorecard. The resulting model is static (every predictor carries a fixed, interpretable coefficient) and thus capable of withstanding regulatory review or legal challenge — a requirement that purely dynamic ML models cannot satisfy in most regulated lending environments.

In practice, however, the richness we mined from the ML layer was significantly eroded when we moved into the logistic regression decision layer due to collinearity (substantial overlapping variance) between psychometric variables. This meant that when we introduced the ML-derived interaction terms and non-linear composites alongside main effects, the model became unstable. The only path to a stable model was to strip the feature set back to main effects, removing most of the engineered variables the ML phase had identified as predictive. Consequently, the richer representation of borrower behavior was effectively lost in the translation to a stable LR.

The recent issuance of SR 26-2 (April 2026), which revises and supersedes the previous SR 11-7 framework, introduces a more risk-based and proportionate approach to Model Risk Management (MRM). While core expectations for governance, validation, and effective challenge remain firmly in place, the updated guidance provides banking organizations with greater flexibility. This represents a meaningful evolution from the more uniform expectations under SR 11-7, enabling responsible innovation in the use of advanced statistical and non-generative AI and ML techniques.

In this context, the aim of the fourth and final phase of this project is to explore how psychometric signals can be transformed into a regulator-ready decision layer while simultaneously maintaining the feature richness typically associated with advanced ML techniques. By applying hybrid methodologies, this phase demonstrates a practical way to balance predictive power, interpretability, and regulatory compliance in line with the principles outlined in SR 26-2.

Methods

To address the core challenge of balancing feature richness (from the ML discovery phase) with model stability and regulatory explainability, Phase 4 applied a structured sequence of statistical transformations and modelling techniques to the psychometric DRA higher-order factors and their interactions.

The following approaches were tested and evaluated:

  • Standardisation (centering and scaling) of all DRA factors to improve numerical stability and facilitate safe creation of interaction terms.

  • Residualisation (orthogonalisation) — evaluated as an alternative (or complementary) technique to reduce multicollinearity between correlated psychometric dimensions.

  • Interaction Discovery and Controlled Inclusion — using XGBoost for feature importance and SHAP interaction values to identify high-value pairwise interactions, followed by careful shortlisting and testing.

  • Weight of Evidence (WOE) Binning — diagnostically evaluated on both main effects and interactions to assess potential benefits in monotonicity and interpretability.

  • Penalised Logistic Regression to handle remaining multicollinearity while performing automatic feature selection.

  • Bayesian Logistic Regression — fitted as a robustness check using weakly informative priors to validate coefficient stability and provide credible intervals.

  • PD Calibration and Scorecard Development — Platt scaling followed by conversion to a 0–1000 points-based scorecard with six risk rating bands (A to F).

This hybrid pipeline was executed exclusively on the development sample, with all transformations and parameters strictly applied to the hold-out test set to prevent data leakage. The goal was to transform rich ML-derived psychometric signals into a stable, transparent, and regulator-ready logistic regression decision layer.

Data

This analysis uses real psychometric assessment data. The scores have been scaled such that risk reduces as the score increases. Additionally, the scores were mapped to a range of 0 to 100.

To protect intellectual property, the names of the DRA variables have been generalized or renamed in this public version. The underlying constructs and relationships remain representative of the actual assessment.

All financial data (accounts, arrears, age on book, bad outcome) is simulated to match realistic distributions, correlations, and patterns from the original analysis (based on a South African thin-file population). The bad outcome (default, bad = 1) was defined as ≥2 payments missed within 6 months, with an overall bad rate of 24.2% in the final population.

No real candidate or client information is included.

Centering / Standardisation

In the next step, the higher-order DRA factors were standardized (centered and scaled to mean = 0, SD = 1) on the train sample only. The same centering and scaling parameters were then applied to the hold-out test set.

Rationale for Standardisation

Standardisation serves several critical purposes in this hybrid modelling pipeline:

  1. Facilitates stable and interpretable interaction terms

When creating interaction terms, standardisation ensures that the main effects and their interactions are placed on comparable scales. Without standardisation, interaction terms can become highly correlated with their constituent main effects, leading to severe multicollinearity, unstable coefficients, and sign reversals. By standardising first, the resulting interaction terms exhibit much lower collinearity with the main effects, allowing the model to better isolate the unique synergistic effects.

  1. Improves numerical stability and model convergence

Penalised models such as Elastic Net are sensitive to differences in variable scales. Standardisation places all predictors on a common scale, enabling the regularisation penalty to treat all features more equitably.

  1. Enhances coefficient interpretability

After standardisation, each coefficient represents the change in log-odds of default associated with a one standard deviation increase in that predictor (or interaction), making results easier to interpret and compare across variables.

This step is essential for successfully carrying forward the rich interaction patterns discovered by ML techniques into a stable, regulator-friendly logistic regression framework.

Residualisation (orthogonalisation)

As an alternative (or complementary) technique to manage potential multicollinearity between correlated psychometric factors, residualisation (also known as orthogonalisation) can be applied. This method involves regressing one variable on another and retaining the residuals, which represent the unique variance not explained by the reference variable.

In this project, residualisation was not applied to the higher-order DRA factors. Diagnostic checks revealed low pairwise correlations and very low Variance Inflation Factors (all VIF < 1.25). This indicated that these variables already captured sufficiently distinct dimensions, making further orthogonalisation unnecessary.

However, residualisation remains a powerful and commonly used approach in psychometric credit scoring when higher overlap exists and helps isolate unique predictive contributions while preserving interpretability.

Discovery of Feature Interactions and Non-Linear Relationships

The SHAP Interaction Plot below displays the mean absolute SHAP interaction value for each term.

  • Bars showing a single variable name (e.g., R_HO_EM2_CO) represent the main effect contribution.

  • Bars showing two variable names separated by a colon (e.g., R_HO_JD_CM:R_HO_EM1_ES) represent the pairwise interaction strength between those two variables.

The height of each bar indicates how much that term (main effect or interaction) contributes, on average, to shifting the model’s prediction (log-odds of default) across all observations. Higher bars mean stronger predictive importance, including synergistic effects that only appear when two variables are considered together.

This plot is particularly valuable because it isolates the additional predictive power coming from interactions, helping us avoid adding weak or redundant terms that could de-stabilise the logistic regression.

Models

Elastic Net Logistic Regression

Elastic Net is a regularized extension of traditional logistic regression that combines the strengths of Ridge (L2) and Lasso (L1) penalization. It adds a penalty term to the loss function that simultaneously:

  • Shrinks unstable or noisy coefficients toward zero, and

  • Performs automatic variable selection by setting some coefficients exactly to zero

The mixing parameter alpha (set to 0.5 in this case) controls the balance between L1 and L2 penalties, while the regularization strength (lambda) is chosen automatically via cross-validation. This makes Elastic Net particularly suitable for high-dimensional or correlated predictor sets, such as psychometric factors and their interactions, while maintaining full interpretability of the final coefficients.

Binning & Weight of Evidence

Weight of Evidence (WOE) binning converts continuous or categorical variables into discrete bins and replaces each bin with its corresponding WOE value.

WOE transformation offers several advantages:

  • Handles non-linearity and non-monotonic relationships naturally.

  • Produces monotonic, interpretable predictors that are highly suitable for regulatory logistic regression models.

  • Facilitates easy scorecard point allocation in later stages.

Diagnostic Evaluation of Weight of Evidence (WOE) Binning

To determine whether this technique would improve model performance or interpretability, a systematic WOE binning diagnostic was performed on the full set of predictors (i.e., all standardised higher-order DRA factors and the 6 interaction terms).

For each predictor, the scorecard package was used to automatically bin the variable using the ChiMerge algorithm. Information Value (IV) was calculated both on the original continuous variable and after WOE transformation. Additionally, the number of direction changes in the WOE trend was assessed to evaluate monotonicity. A positive Benefit Score would indicate that WOE binning improves predictive power. All analysis was conducted exclusively on the training sample.

results <- list()
  
  for (var in vars) {
    suppressWarnings(suppressMessages({
      
      bins <- woebin(data,
                     y = target_var,
                     x = var,
                     binning_method = "chimerge",
                     positive = 1,
                     print_info = FALSE)   
      
      iv_raw <- iv(data, y = target_var, x = var)$info_value[1]
      iv_woe <- bins[[1]]$total_iv[1]
      
      woe_values <- bins[[1]]$woe
      direction_changes <- sum(diff(sign(diff(woe_values))) != 0)
      
      results[[var]] <- data.frame(
        Variable          = var,
        IV_Raw            = round(iv_raw, 3),
        IV_WOE            = round(iv_woe, 3),
        Benefit           = round(iv_woe - iv_raw, 3),
        Direction_Changes = direction_changes,
        Num_Bins          = nrow(bins[[1]])
      )
    }))
  }
  
  # Only show final summary
  summary_df <- do.call(rbind, results)
  summary_df <- summary_df[order(summary_df$Benefit), ]   # worst to best
  invisible(summary_df)

Bayesian Logistic Regression

As a final robustness check within Stage 4, a Bayesian logistic regression was fitted using the same standardised higher-order DRA factors and interaction terms. Weakly informative normal priors (N(0, 2.5)) were applied to all coefficients and the intercept. The model was estimated using Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling with 4 chains. The model showed excellent convergence.

This Bayesian approach offers several advantages:

  • Naturally incorporates uncertainty through posterior distributions.

  • Provides credible intervals that are more intuitive than frequentist confidence intervals.

  • Regularising priors help stabilize estimates in the presence of correlated predictors.

### Background and Aim 

In the third phase of this project, we deployed the ML-informed Logistic Regression (LR) — a hybrid development methodology that uses Machine Learning (ML) techniques during the feature engineering phase to discover which non-linear effects and feature interactions carry genuine predictive signal. Those discovered patterns were then encoded as explicit, fixed terms inside a production logistic regression, preserving full coefficient transparency and regulatory auditability.

In principle, this approach gives us the best of both worlds: The feature engineering intelligence of ML combined with the regulatory defensibility of a traditional scorecard. The resulting model is static (every predictor carries a fixed, interpretable coefficient) and thus capable of withstanding regulatory review or legal challenge — a requirement that purely dynamic ML models cannot satisfy in most regulated lending environments.

In practice, however, the richness we mined from the ML layer was significantly eroded when we moved into the logistic regression decision layer due to collinearity (substantial overlapping variance) between psychometric variables. This meant that when we introduced the ML-derived interaction terms and non-linear composites alongside main effects, the model became unstable. The only path to a stable model was to strip the feature set back to main effects, removing most of the engineered variables the ML phase had identified as predictive. Consequently, the richer representation of borrower behavior was effectively lost in the translation to a stable LR.

The recent issuance of **SR 26-2** (April 2026), which revises and supersedes the previous SR 11-7 framework, introduces a more risk-based and proportionate approach to Model Risk Management (MRM). While core expectations for governance, validation, and effective challenge remain firmly in place, the updated guidance provides banking organizations with greater flexibility. This represents a meaningful evolution from the more uniform expectations under SR 11-7, enabling responsible innovation in the use of advanced statistical and non-generative AI and ML techniques.

In this context, the aim of the fourth and final phase of this project is to explore how psychometric signals can be transformed into a regulator-ready decision layer while simultaneously maintaining the feature richness typically associated with advanced ML techniques. By applying hybrid methodologies, this phase demonstrates a practical way to balance predictive power, interpretability, and regulatory compliance in line with the principles outlined in SR 26-2.

### Methods

To address the core challenge of balancing feature richness (from the ML discovery phase) with model stability and regulatory explainability, Phase 4 applied a structured sequence of statistical transformations and modelling techniques to the psychometric DRA higher-order factors and their interactions.

The following approaches were tested and evaluated:

- Standardisation (centering and scaling) of all DRA factors to improve numerical stability and facilitate safe creation of interaction terms.

- Residualisation (orthogonalisation) — evaluated as an alternative (or complementary) technique to reduce multicollinearity between correlated psychometric dimensions.

- Interaction Discovery and Controlled Inclusion — using XGBoost for feature importance and SHAP interaction values to identify high-value pairwise interactions, followed by careful shortlisting and testing.

- Weight of Evidence (WOE) Binning — diagnostically evaluated on both main effects and interactions to assess potential benefits in monotonicity and interpretability.

- Penalised Logistic Regression to handle remaining multicollinearity while performing automatic feature selection.

- Bayesian Logistic Regression — fitted as a robustness check using weakly informative priors to validate coefficient stability and provide credible intervals.

- PD Calibration and Scorecard Development — Platt scaling followed by conversion to a 0–1000 points-based scorecard with six risk rating bands (A to F).

This hybrid pipeline was executed exclusively on the development sample, with all transformations and parameters strictly applied to the hold-out test set to prevent data leakage. The goal was to transform rich ML-derived psychometric signals into a stable, transparent, and regulator-ready logistic regression decision layer.

### Data

This analysis uses real **psychometric assessment data**. The scores have been scaled such that risk reduces as the score increases. Additionally, the scores were mapped to a range of 0 to 100.

To protect intellectual property, the names of the DRA variables have been generalized or renamed in this public version. The underlying constructs and relationships remain representative of the actual assessment.

**All financial data** (accounts, arrears, age on book, bad outcome) is simulated to match realistic distributions, correlations, and patterns from the original analysis (based on a South African thin-file population). The bad outcome (default, bad = 1) was defined as ≥2 payments missed within 6 months, with an overall bad rate of 24.2% in the final population.

#### Centering / Standardisation 

In the next step, the higher-order DRA factors were standardized (centered and scaled to mean = 0, SD = 1) on the train sample only. The same centering and scaling parameters were then applied to the hold-out test set. 

**Rationale for Standardisation** 

Standardisation serves several critical purposes in this hybrid modelling pipeline:

1. Facilitates stable and interpretable interaction terms

When creating interaction terms, standardisation ensures that the main effects and their interactions are placed on comparable scales. Without standardisation, interaction terms can become highly correlated with their constituent main effects, leading to severe multicollinearity, unstable coefficients, and sign reversals. By standardising first, the resulting interaction terms exhibit much lower collinearity with the main effects, allowing the model to better isolate the unique synergistic effects.

2. Improves numerical stability and model convergence

Penalised models such as Elastic Net are sensitive to differences in variable scales. Standardisation places all predictors on a common scale, enabling the regularisation penalty to treat all features more equitably.

3. Enhances coefficient interpretability

After standardisation, each coefficient represents the change in log-odds of default associated with a one standard deviation increase in that predictor (or interaction), making results easier to interpret and compare across variables.

This step is essential for successfully carrying forward the rich interaction patterns discovered by ML techniques into a stable, regulator-friendly logistic regression framework.

#### Residualisation (orthogonalisation)

As an alternative (or complementary) technique to manage potential multicollinearity between correlated psychometric factors, residualisation (also known as orthogonalisation) can be applied. This method involves regressing one variable on another and retaining the residuals, which represent the unique variance not explained by the reference variable.

In this project, residualisation was not applied to the higher-order DRA factors. Diagnostic checks revealed low pairwise correlations and very low Variance Inflation Factors (all VIF < 1.25). This indicated that these variables already captured sufficiently distinct dimensions, making further orthogonalisation unnecessary.

However, residualisation remains a powerful and commonly used approach in psychometric credit scoring when higher overlap exists and helps isolate unique predictive contributions while preserving interpretability.

### Models 

#### Elastic Net Logistic Regression

Elastic Net is a regularized extension of traditional logistic regression that combines the strengths of Ridge (L2) and Lasso (L1) penalization. It adds a penalty term to the loss function that simultaneously:

- Shrinks unstable or noisy coefficients toward zero, and

- Performs automatic variable selection by setting some coefficients exactly to zero 

The mixing parameter alpha (set to 0.5 in this case) controls the balance between L1 and L2 penalties, while the regularization strength (lambda) is chosen automatically via cross-validation. This makes Elastic Net particularly suitable for high-dimensional or correlated predictor sets, such as psychometric factors and their interactions, while maintaining full interpretability of the final coefficients.

#### Binning & Weight of Evidence 

Weight of Evidence (WOE) binning converts continuous or categorical variables into discrete bins and replaces each bin with its corresponding WOE value. 

WOE transformation offers several advantages:

- Handles non-linearity and non-monotonic relationships naturally.

- Produces monotonic, interpretable predictors that are highly suitable for regulatory logistic regression models.

- Facilitates easy scorecard point allocation in later stages.

**Diagnostic Evaluation of Weight of Evidence (WOE) Binning** 

To determine whether this technique would improve model performance or interpretability, a systematic WOE binning diagnostic was performed on the full set of predictors (i.e., all standardised higher-order DRA factors and the 6 interaction terms). 

For each predictor, the `scorecard` package was used to automatically bin the variable using the ChiMerge algorithm. Information Value (IV) was calculated both on the original continuous variable and after WOE transformation. Additionally, the number of direction changes in the WOE trend was assessed to evaluate monotonicity. A positive Benefit Score would indicate that WOE binning improves predictive power. All analysis was conducted exclusively on the training sample. 

```{r results = FALSE}
set.seed(42)

                     "R_HO_JD_CM_x_EM1_ES", 
                     "R_HO_TC3_DI_x_JD_CM",

                     "R_HO_EM1_ES_x_TC1_AG", 
                     "R_HO_JD_CM_x_TC2_OC")

# Diagnostic function
evaluate_woe_potential <- function(data, vars, target_var) {
  
results <- list()
  
  for (var in vars) {
    suppressWarnings(suppressMessages({
      
      bins <- woebin(data,
                     y = target_var,
                     x = var,
                     binning_method = "chimerge",
                     positive = 1,
                     print_info = FALSE)   
      
      iv_raw <- iv(data, y = target_var, x = var)$info_value[1]
      iv_woe <- bins[[1]]$total_iv[1]
      
      woe_values <- bins[[1]]$woe
      direction_changes <- sum(diff(sign(diff(woe_values))) != 0)
      
      results[[var]] <- data.frame(
        Variable          = var,
        IV_Raw            = round(iv_raw, 3),
        IV_WOE            = round(iv_woe, 3),
        Benefit           = round(iv_woe - iv_raw, 3),
        Direction_Changes = direction_changes,
        Num_Bins          = nrow(bins[[1]])
      )
    }))
  }
  
  # Only show final summary
  summary_df <- do.call(rbind, results)
  summary_df <- summary_df[order(summary_df$Benefit), ]   # worst to best
  invisible(summary_df)
}

woe_full_eval <- evaluate_woe_potential(data_train_final, full_predictors, target_var)
```

#### Bayesian Logistic Regression

As a final robustness check within Stage 4, a Bayesian logistic regression was fitted using the same standardised higher-order DRA factors and interaction terms. Weakly informative normal priors (N(0, 2.5)) were applied to all coefficients and the intercept. The model was estimated using Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling with 4 chains. The model showed excellent convergence. 

This Bayesian approach offers several advantages:

- Naturally incorporates uncertainty through posterior distributions.

- Provides credible intervals that are more intuitive than frequentist confidence intervals.

- Regularising priors help stabilize estimates in the presence of correlated predictors.

### PD Calibration and Scorecard Development 

The final Elastic Net logistic regression model was selected as the production model. Predicted probabilities were calibrated using Platt scaling (logistic calibration) to ensure well-calibrated Probability of Default (PD) estimates. A traditional scorecard was created by scaling the calibrated PDs to a 0–1000 score range, where higher scores indicate lower risk.

```{r}
# 1. Raw predictions
pred_train_raw <- predict(final_model, newx = X_train, s = best_lambda, type = "response")[,1]
pred_test_raw  <- predict(final_model, newx = X_test,  s = best_lambda, type = "response")[,1]

# 2. Calibration model
cal_model <- glm(y_train ~ pred_train_raw, family = binomial(link = "logit"))

# 3. Calibrated probabilities 
newdata_train <- data.frame(pred_train_raw = pred_train_raw)
newdata_test  <- data.frame(pred_train_raw = pred_test_raw)   

cal_train_prob <- predict(cal_model, newdata = newdata_train, type = "response")
cal_test_prob  <- predict(cal_model, newdata = newdata_test,  type = "response")

# 4. Assign to data
data_train_final <- as.data.frame(data_train_final)
data_test_final  <- as.data.frame(data_test_final)

data_train_final[["PD_Calibrated"]] <- cal_train_prob
data_test_final[["PD_Calibrated"]]  <- cal_test_prob

data_train_final[["Score"]] <- round(1000 * (1 - cal_train_prob))
data_test_final[["Score"]]  <- round(1000 * (1 - cal_test_prob))
```

The final scorecard was segmented into six risk rating bands (A to F), with Band A representing the lowest risk and Band F the highest risk. The banding structure demonstrates strong rank-ordering power, with observed bad rates increasing monotonically from Band A (~10–11%) to Band F (~36–38%) in both the development and hold-out test samples.

Importantly, the performance is highly consistent between the development and test sets, indicating good generalisation and stable calibration. This monotonic relationship and out-of-sample stability support the use of these bands for clear business decision rules.
